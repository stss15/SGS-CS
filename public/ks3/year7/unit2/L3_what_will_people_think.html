Concepts & Skills

Concept: Online Reputation

How others perceive you based on your digital actions, posts, and behaviour across platforms.

Skill: Critical Judgement

Evaluating online situations and making responsible decisions about how to behave in different contexts.

Lesson Objectives

  • Identify ways online behaviour changes across different platforms.
  • Explain how digital identity can evolve over time.
  • Recognise when behaviour needs to adapt for safety, privacy, or wellbeing.
  • Make reasoned judgements about what's acceptable online.

The Big Question

Would you behave differently online if everyone could see exactly who you are?

Task: Discussion: Think of a time you saw someone behave badly online. Would they have done it in person?

Core Keywords

  • Online Reputation — how others view you based on your digital actions.
  • Context Collapse — when different audiences unexpectedly see the same content.
  • Anonymity — hiding your identity when interacting online.
  • Digital Citizenship — behaving responsibly and respectfully online.
  • Online Norms — unwritten rules about how people behave on different platforms.

Context Collapse

When different parts of your life unexpectedly see the same content.

Example: A joke you share with friends gets seen by your parents, teachers, and future employers.

"On social media, everyone is your audience."

Who Might See Your Post?

Intended Audience

  • Close friends
  • School peers
  • Family members

Unintended Audience

  • Future employers
  • University admissions
  • Strangers who screenshot
  • People who share further

Why Does This Matter?

Career Impact

70% of employers check social media before hiring. Your digital footprint follows you.

Relationships

How you treat people online affects how they see you in real life.

Key Point: Your online behaviour creates a permanent record of who you appear to be.

Key Takeaway

The Golden Rule of Digital Citizenship:

"Don't say anything online that you wouldn't say to someone's face."

Being anonymous doesn't make hurtful behaviour okay — it just means you're hiding.

Common Misconceptions

Let's check some myths about online behaviour:

Press Down to check each one

Misconception #1

"If I delete something, it's gone forever."

❌ FALSE

Screenshots, cached copies, and archives mean nothing is truly deleted. Once it's online, assume it's permanent.

Misconception #2

"It's just a joke — people shouldn't be so sensitive."

❌ FALSE

Intent doesn't equal impact. A "joke" can still cause real harm, and you're responsible for how your words affect others.

Misconception #3

"Nobody important will ever see my posts."

❌ FALSE

Future employers, university admissions, and even strangers can find your digital footprint. Assume everything is public.

Misconception #4

"Being anonymous online protects me from consequences."

❌ FALSE

Anonymity is not immunity. Serious cases can be traced by police, and the harm you cause is still real even if you hide.

Question 1

Adapting your behaviour online means pretending to be someone else.

Answer: False

Adaptation is about context and safety, not being dishonest about who you are.

Question 2

What you post online can affect your reputation years later.

Answer: True

Digital footprints are permanent — universities and employers check social media.

Question 3

Being anonymous online means you can say anything without consequences.

Answer: False

Online actions can still hurt people, and anonymity does not protect you legally.
